Cold email used to mean reaching out to prospects you'd never spoken to before.

Now? Most "cold" email is just bad email. Wrong person, wrong message, wrong time — blasted at scale.

The channel isn't dead. The approach is.

What's working in 2026:
- Smaller, tighter lists built around real buying signals
- Messaging that speaks to one specific problem, not a general pitch
- Following up with value, not just "just checking in"
- Treating deliverability as a foundation, not an afterthought

The fundamentals haven't changed. The execution bar has just gone up.

They immediately send a calendar link and nothing else.

What to do instead:
- Acknowledge what they said

- Then offer the next step

You earned the reply. Don't waste it with a lazy response.

If you handed me a client with a completely cold market and said "book meetings in 30 days" — here's exactly what I'd do:

Step 1 — Nail the ICP. Not "SaaS companies." Something like: Series A SaaS, 20-100 employees, using Salesforce, hiring SDRs right now.

Step 2 — Build the list around a trigger. Funding rounds, new hires, tech stack changes. People buying when something just changed.

Step 3 — Write one email. Short. Specific. One problem. One CTA. No fluff.

Step 4 — Send it from a warmed domain. Not your main one.

Step 5 — Follow up 3 times with value, not pressure.

That's it. No magic. Just specificity at every step.

## Post 4 — Cold email sequence 101
Cold email sequence 101:

Every email in the sequence needs to:
- Be well-targeted
- Be 2-3 sentences max
- Have a no-brainer offer
- Be personalized and relevant
- Have a soft CTA offering a sales asset

Each sequence should be 4 steps (1 cold, 3 follow-up):
- Email 1 — Offer + Value
- Email 2 — Follow-up + Value/Sales Asset
- Email 3 — Follow-up + Value/Sales Asset
- Email 4 — Break-up

With the right cold emails, right sequence settings, and right deliverability hygiene — results follow.

## Post 5 — The Obvious Yes Method
The easiest way to book 20-30% more meetings with cold email (without hyper-personalizing each email). I call it "The Obvious Yes Method."

If your email can't check each of these boxes, don't send it:
- Short
- Valuable
- Targeted
- Personalized
- Extremely easy to read

Subject lines that consistently work:
1. quick question
2. {name}, thoughts?
3. question about {service}

Always add unsubscribe requests to your DNC list — it protects your domain reputation and is the right thing to do.
